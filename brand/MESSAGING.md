# Hometown Painting — Messaging Playbook

**Read this before writing or rewriting ANY website copy, page, or ad.** It distills three
strategy docs (full versions in `brand/source/`): a StoryBrand narrative playbook, our Ideal
Customer Profile, and verbatim customer-frustration research from the OKC painting market.

The one rule everything else serves: **the customer is the Hero; Hometown is the Guide.**
We never make the site about us, our awards, or our "passion." We make it about the homeowner's
problem, their fear, and how we hand them control back.

---

## 1. Who we're talking to (the Hero)

An established OKC-metro homeowner, **45–65**, dual-income professional or near-retirement,
household income **$100K–$200K+**, in a **$300K–$550K** brick/stone home that's **10–25 years old**
with **builder-grade exterior paint that's visibly failing**. They own the home, have lived there
7–20 years, and plan to stay — possibly through retirement.

**They are nervous, not excited.** They've watched the paint fail for a year or two and finally
accepted they can't ignore it. By the time they reach the site they've already imagined the
worst. **Price is almost never their #1 concern — trust is.** They will pay more for the company
that makes them feel safe, heard, and in control.

Write to *that* person. Not to a bargain hunter, not to a 28-year-old flipper, not to "everyone."

## 2. The core insight: the meta-fear is LOSS OF CONTROL

86% of customer pain points in the research trace to one fear: **"I lose control when I hire a
painter."** (Oklahoma's AG confirms contractor fraud is the #1 consumer complaint in the state.)
Every reassurance on the site should hand a piece of control back.

### The five root fears — and the exact proof that resolves each

Weave these resolutions into copy wherever relevant. Lead with the fear (agitate briefly), then
resolve it. **Show the proof, don't just claim the virtue.**

| # | Root fear (Hero's words) | What resolves it (our real proof) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (41%) | "They'll damage my home and I'll be powerless to stop it." | Vetted, long-tenured crews; protection & cleanup protocols; **Matt's personal walk-through**; fully insured (liability + workers' comp). |
| 2 (31%) | "They'll waste my time and never show up." | On-the-spot proposals; clear scheduling; daily communication; a track record of doing what we say. |
| 3 (28%) | "I can't tell who's legitimate and who's a scam." | **130+ verified 5-star Google reviews**; transparent published pricing; named products (Sherwin-Williams, Wood Defender); written warranty. |
| 4 (24%) | "Once they have my money, I'll have no recourse." | **25% deposit only, balance after the walk-through**; written warranty; Matt's personal cell. |
| 5 (17%) | "Untrained strangers will be in my home unsupervised." | Long-tenured crew (not a rotating cast of subs); insured; **the owner is personally involved on every job**. |

### The competitor complaints we quietly answer
The market's three dominant frustrations (from real Trustpilot/BBB reviews of CertaPro, Five Star,
etc.): **(1) paint fails early / damages the home and they won't own it → you pay twice** (50%),
**(2) reliability collapse — ghosting, no-shows, estimates that never arrive** (25%), **(3) financial
exposure — deposit games, final-price shock, paying before completion** (25%). Our positioning is
the mirror image of each. We don't name competitors; we just be the opposite.

### Product Promise (a north-star line — adapt, don't necessarily quote verbatim)
> *You don't pay the final dollar until we walk it with you — clean, protected, and right — and if
> our work fails, we fix it.*

## 3. What our best customers actually value (rank order — put these first)

1. **Trustworthiness & accountability** — does what they say, answers the phone when there's a
   problem (in 67% of positive reviews).
2. **Owner involvement** — Matt personally estimates, checks the work, is reachable (57%).
3. **Reliability** — shows up and finishes when promised (52%).
4. **Respect for the home** — protects belongings, cleans up (43%).
5. **Quality of the finished product** — matters, but they mention it *last*; the *experience* of
   being kept safe and informed is what they remember.

Note: price appears in good reviews only as "fair"/"reasonable," never "cheap." Don't compete on
cheap — compete on trust and peace of mind.

## 4. Voice & StoryBrand mechanics

- **Guide energy = empathy + authority.** Empathy: "We know how it feels to have watched the paint
  peel for two years and dread the phone calls." Authority: "Here's exactly how our process works,
  and here's the warranty in writing." Never one without the other.
- **Zero cognitive load.** Bumper sticker beats the book. Short, concrete, plain. Cut jargon,
  cut "clever," cut hedging. "If it's peeling, it's time. Call us."
- **Hook by naming the problem**, then briefly agitate it (the physical + emotional cost), *then*
  offer the answer. A page that opens with our history invites the reader to tune out.
- **The PEACE beats** for any hero/CTA section: **P**roblem → **E**mpathy → **A**nswer → **C**hange
  → **E**nd result. (WAB example: "Worried about money? We get it. Download the app. Get good with
  money. So you never worry again.")
- **Oneliner formula = Problem + Product + Result.** e.g. *"Oklahoma weather wrecks exterior paint
  and most homeowners dread hiring a painter. Hometown Painting handles the whole repaint with a
  written warranty and a walk-through before you pay the balance — so your home is protected and
  you're never left guessing."* Good under an H1, in meta descriptions, in the footer.
- **The About page is an "Origin of Empathy," not a résumé.** Formula: The Hole (the struggle) →
  The Tool (what we built) → The Mission (why rescuing others became the point) → The Transformation.
  Matt learning the trade from his grandfather Art belongs here *as proof of competence and care*,
  not as nostalgia for its own right.
- **Repeat the soundbites relentlessly** across pages. Consistency > novelty.

## 5. Attribution for real testimonials

Pull only REAL reviews from `migration/reviews/` (see CLAUDE.md), matched to the page's city/service.
Attribution convention: **First name + last initial, role, city** —
> — **Sally V., Homeowner · Edmond**    ·    — **Susan G., Office Manager · Oklahoma City** (commercial)

Prefer reviews that themselves resolve a root fear (mention communication, cleanup, showing up,
Matt personally, the walk-through) — those do double duty.

## 6. Geography priority (where the ideal customer concentrates)

- **Tier 1 (home base):** Yukon, Mustang, SW Edmond — our strongest referral networks; less
  competition. Deepest content + proof here.
- **Tier 2 (growth):** Edmond (central/north), Nichols Hills, Deer Creek — higher values, more
  competition (CertaPro, Legacy Painting 4.9★/203 reviews). Build review volume + local content.
- **Tier 3 (specialty):** Mesta Park, Heritage Hills, Crown Heights — historic homes, premium
  pricing, almost no competition. We already have a Mesta Park page + a project video.

## 7. Who we are NOT for (don't write to these)

Price shoppers, unrealistic-expectation customers (want a $15K restoration for $5K), abusive
customers, landlords/flippers, and interior-only discount seekers. Copy should gently *self-select
these out* — leaning into warranty, process, and premium proof naturally repels the bargain hunter.
Never add "cheapest," "budget," "lowest price" language to attract volume.

## 8. Quick do / don't

**Do:** open with the homeowner's problem; agitate briefly; resolve with concrete proof; name the
walk-through-before-final-payment and the written warranty often; keep Matt personally present in
the story; use real local reviews; short sentences; specific numbers (5-year warranty, 25% deposit,
130+ reviews, 7 years in business).

**Don't:** make Hometown the hero; list awards/passion first; use jargon or "clever" taglines;
promise services we don't offer (see CLAUDE.md scope); invent or generic-ify testimonials; compete
on price; bury the CTA; write to "everyone."
