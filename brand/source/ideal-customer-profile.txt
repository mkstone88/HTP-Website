Purpose of This Document
This document defines the specific type of customer Hometown Painting serves best — and equally important, the types we don’t. Every business decision flows from this: which leads we pursue, which neighborhoods we market to, what language we use on our website, how we present our proposals, and when we say no.
This profile is built from real data: our 2025 P&L, marketing dashboard, 120+ customer reviews, competitive landscape analysis, verbatim customer frustrations from across the OKC market, and Matt’s direct experience running estimates and managing projects for seven years.
The core principle: We would rather serve fewer customers exceptionally well at full margin than serve many customers at a discount. Every customer we take at the wrong price or the wrong fit costs us money, time, and energy that could have gone to a customer who values what we do.

THE IDEAL CUSTOMER AT A GLANCE

Our ideal customer is an established homeowner in the OKC metro — typically 45 to 65 years old, dual-income professional or approaching retirement — whose home’s exterior paint is visibly failing after 10 to 20 years of Oklahoma weather. They have the budget to pay for quality work. Their primary concern is not price — it’s finding a contractor they can trust with their biggest investment. They’ve either been burned before or they’ve heard enough horror stories to be nervous about the process. They find us through Google search, neighbor referrals, or past experience with our company. They close at 2–3x the rate of paid advertising leads and generate significantly higher job values.

SECTION 2: PSYCHOGRAPHICS

How They Think and What They Fear
Understanding the psychology of our ideal customer is more important than their demographics. Two homeowners with identical incomes and identical homes will make completely different hiring decisions based on their mindset, past experiences, and emotional state. Our ideal customer shares specific psychological traits that make them receptive to what we offer.

Their Emotional State When They Contact Us
They’re nervous, not excited. Our market research analyzed 29 independent pain-point data points across Google, Angi, Yelp, BBB, Nextdoor, Houzz, Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor, Trustpilot, local news, and the Oklahoma Attorney General’s office. The dominant finding: 86% of all collected data points connect to a single meta-fear: “I lose control when I hire a painter.” The Oklahoma AG confirms that construction and contractor fraud is the #1 consumer complaint in the state.
Our ideal customer is not calling us because they’re thrilled to paint their house. They’re calling because they’ve been watching the paint fail for a year or two and they’ve finally accepted they can’t ignore it anymore. By the time they pick up the phone, they’ve already imagined three worst-case scenarios.

What They Actually Value (In Order)
When asked “What are you looking for in a painting contractor?” our best customers consistently prioritize these attributes. This ranking comes from analyzing 21 positive Hometown reviews and cross-referencing with stated preferences during estimates.
Trustworthiness and accountability. Someone who does what they say and answers the phone when there’s a problem. Mentioned in 67% of positive reviews (as “communication,” “responsiveness,” “kept us informed”).
Reliability. Shows up when promised. Finishes when promised. No surprises. Mentioned in 52% of reviews.
Respect for the home. Protects belongings, cleans up, treats the house like it matters. Mentioned in 43% of reviews.
Owner involvement. The fact that Matt personally comes out, personally checks the work, and is personally reachable. Mentioned in 57% of reviews.
Quality of the finished product. This matters, but it’s often mentioned last — because the process experience (trust, communication, reliability) is what they remember most.
Note: Price is almost never mentioned as a primary criterion by our best customers. When it appears in reviews, it’s phrased as “fair” or “reasonable” — not “cheap” or “affordable.” The ideal customer is willing to pay more; they need to understand why.

Their Decision-Making Process
Our ideal customer doesn’t impulse-buy a $6,000 paint job. Their decision process typically takes 1–4 weeks from first contact to signing, and follows a predictable pattern:
Trigger: They’ve noticed the paint failing for 6–24 months. Something pushes them to act — an upcoming event, a neighbor’s home getting painted, a storm that made the damage worse, or simply reaching a breaking point.
Research: They Google “painters near me” or “exterior painting [city].” They read reviews carefully. They ask neighbors or colleagues. They are actively looking for reasons to trust — and reasons to eliminate.
Comparison: They get 2–3 estimates. They are comparing the experience of the estimate process as much as the price. The company that made them feel heard, informed, and safe wins — even at a higher price.
Decision: They choose based on confidence and comfort, not spreadsheet comparison. If both spouses were present during the estimate and both felt good, the close rate is dramatically higher.

SECTION 5: WHO WE DON’T SERVE

The Anti-ICP: Customers We Should Decline or Avoid
Knowing who NOT to serve is just as important as knowing who to serve. Every hour spent on a bad-fit customer is an hour stolen from a good-fit customer. Every discounted job pulls down your margin and trains the market to expect lower prices. These are the customer profiles that consistently produce poor outcomes for Hometown Painting.

The Price Shopper
How to spot them: First question is “How much?” before you’ve even seen the house. They’ve already gotten 3–5 quotes. They mention the cheapest one within the first few minutes. They found you through Facebook lead forms or a generic “cheapest painters” search.
Why they’re a bad fit: They will never value what differentiates you. Your warranty, your prep process, your communication, your crews — none of it matters to them. They want the lowest number. If you win the job, it’s because you discounted, which means you’ll lose margin. If something goes wrong, they’ll be your most difficult customer.
What to do: Give them your standard proposal at full price. If they push hard on price, let them go. Do not discount to win a price shopper.

The Unrealistic Expectation Customer
How to spot them: Their home is in severe disrepair but they’re expecting a mid-range price. They describe scope that would cost $15K but have a $5K budget in mind. They use language like “sand everything down to bare wood” for a standard repaint price.
Why they’re a bad fit: Misaligned expectations create conflict. If you take the job at their budget, you can’t deliver what they’re imagining and they’ll be unhappy. If you price it correctly, they’ll think you’re gouging them. This was the worst-customer scenario Matt described — a customer who paid $5K and expected a $15K restoration.
What to do: Set clear expectations during the estimate. “What you’re describing is a full restoration, which typically runs $12K–15K. What we can do within a $5K–7K range is a professional repaint that addresses the worst areas. Would you like me to scope both options?” If they can’t align expectations with budget, walk away.

The Abusive Customer
How to spot them: Disrespectful to you during the estimate. Demanding in a way that goes beyond “high expectations” into demeaning. Uses language that signals they will mistreat your crew.
Why they’re a bad fit: A core brand value is that we respect our people. A customer who will be abusive to Ivan and the crew is not a customer we want, regardless of the job size. This is a line Hometown does not cross.
What to do: Politely decline the project. “I don’t think we’re the best fit for what you’re looking for. I’d be happy to recommend another company that might be a better match.”

The Landlord / Flipper (Low-Quality Volume)
How to spot them: They own rental properties or are renovating to sell. They want the cheapest possible paint, minimum prep, and the fastest turnaround. They view painting as a commodity expense, not an investment.
Why they’re a bad fit: These jobs require volume pricing to win and produce thin or negative margins. They don’t generate reviews (tenants don’t leave reviews for the landlord’s painter), they don’t generate referrals to other ideal customers, and they fill your schedule during peak season when you should be serving premium clients.
What to do: If the project is large enough to be profitable at full price, take it. Otherwise, decline. “Our pricing reflects the premium materials and prep work we include. For a more budget-oriented approach, I’d recommend [referring to a known budget painter].”

The Interior-Only Discount Seeker
How to spot them: They want interior painting, they’re comparing 4+ quotes, and their primary criterion is price. Interior projects are already your lowest-margin work, and these customers push the margin even lower.
Why they’re a bad fit: Your 2025 data shows interior projects are labor-intensive and you’ve been lowering prices to compete. This creates a downward spiral: discount to win, suffer on margin, fill the calendar with low-profit work, miss opportunities for higher-margin exterior projects.
What to do: Price interior projects at full 50%+ gross margin. If the customer balks, let them go to That 1 Painter or Fixable Painting. Only take interior work at full price, ideally from existing exterior customers who already trust you and are less price-sensitive.

SECTION 6: QUALIFYING LEADS BEFORE THE ESTIMATE

The Pre-Estimate Qualification Check
Not every lead deserves an estimate. Every estimate you run costs roughly 90 minutes of Matt’s time plus fuel, which at a $500K business is worth approximately $150–$200 in opportunity cost. Running 15 estimates to close 3 jobs means 12 estimates were wasted — roughly $1,800–$2,400/month in lost productivity. Better qualification upfront means fewer but higher-quality appointments, higher close rates, and more time for the work that actually builds the business.
During the confirmation call or initial contact, gather enough information to determine fit. Here are the qualifying signals:

A single red flag doesn’t disqualify a lead. But three or more red flags together suggest this lead is unlikely to close at a profitable price and Matt’s time would be better spent elsewhere.

HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Making This Actionable
This ICP document is a living reference, not a one-time read. Here’s how it should be used:
Before every estimate: Does this lead match the ICP? Check their source channel, geography, and any qualifying signals from the initial call.
When building marketing campaigns: Target Tier 1 and 2 geographies. Use the psychographic insights to write ad copy that speaks to their fears and values.
When onboarding a salesperson: This document is their first training material. Before they learn the Sales SOP, they need to understand who they’re selling to and why those people buy.
When reviewing monthly performance: Track close rates by lead source. If any channel’s close rate drops below 25%, evaluate whether it’s attracting the ICP or attracting noise.
When something feels off about a lead: Reference the Anti-ICP section. If the customer matches two or more anti-profiles, give a full-price proposal and be prepared to walk away.

The Bottom Line
Our ideal customer isn’t defined by demographics alone. They’re defined by a specific emotional state (nervous, seeking trust), a specific decision-making approach (research-driven, value-over-price), and a specific way of finding us (intentional search or personal referral). When we attract these customers and serve them well, they pay full price, leave five-star reviews, and send their neighbors. That’s the engine that takes Hometown Painting from $500K to $5M.
